01 — Molecular Handedness

The Amino Acid Mirror

Chirality pervades nature far below the scale of our opposable thumbs. A molecule is chiral when it cannot be superimposed on its mirror image. Your left and right hands share this property: same structure, same atoms, but not identical. The distinction is not cosmetic. It is the difference between a drug that heals and one that harms.

Life on Earth made a choice. Of the twenty standard amino acids, almost all occur exclusively in their left-handed (L-enantiomer) form. Right-handed amino acids are chemically viable. Nature simply does not use them. DNA winds the other way: a right-handed double helix, with the lone exception of Z-DNA, which forms reluctantly and under duress.

The Castle Staircase

Medieval spiral staircases wound clockwise ascending. A right-handed defender retreating upward could swing freely. The attacker's blade met the central column. Only an army of left-handers could fight on equal terms. If they won, they inherited a fortress that offered them no advantage in return.

Homochirality in biology carries the same flavour. Once one handedness dominates, it excludes the other through structural incompatibility rather than energetic superiority.

Meteorites deliver amino acids to Earth. More arrive left-handed than right. The Rosetta mission to comet 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko searched for this asymmetry at the source. The question persists: was the bias seeded by circularly polarised light in the protostellar nebula, or by some deeper asymmetry written into the laws themselves?

02 — Electromagnetic Chirality

Polarisation and the Helix of Light

An incandescent bulb spews photons with randomly oriented electric fields. No preferred direction. But select a single propagating wave and the electric field vector traces a definite path. If it oscillates along a fixed axis, the light is linearly polarised. If it rotates as the wave advances, the tip of the field vector scribes a helix.

The convention: looking toward the source, if the electric field rotates counterclockwise the light is right-circularly polarised. The helix it scribes is palindromic in time. Reverse time and the propagation direction flips, but the helicity does not. The helix reads the same forwards and backwards.

Parity is different. Invert the spatial coordinates and right-circular becomes left-circular. Helicity flips sign under P. This is the operational definition of chirality for radiation: a property that changes under spatial inversion but not under time reversal.

Optical Rotation

When linearly polarised light traverses a chiral medium, the two circular components travel at different speeds. The plane of polarisation rotates. If one component is also preferentially absorbed, the phenomenon is circular dichroism. Both effects are diagnostic of molecular handedness.

03 — Discrete Symmetries

C, P, T — and Their Failure

Three candidate symmetries of nature. Each plausible on its own. Each broken.

C
Charge Conjugation
Replace every particle with its antiparticle. Flip charge and all internal quantum numbers.
P
Parity
Invert spatial coordinates. Exchange left with right. x → −x
T
Time Reversal
Replace t with −t. Reverse momenta and angular momenta.

Wu's 1957 experiment with cobalt-60 demonstrated that parity is not conserved in beta decay. Nature distinguishes left from right. The neutrinos produced were always left-handed. Antineutrinos always right-handed. This was not a statistical preference. It was absolute.

The combination CP was then proposed as the true symmetry. In 1964, kaon decay proved otherwise. CP violation is real and has since been confirmed in B-meson systems. Only the triple combination CPT appears to hold universally, a requirement linked to the Lorentz invariance of quantum field theory.

A Thermodynamic Tension

If CP is violated but CPT is exact, then T must also be violated. This sits uneasily beside the time-symmetry of fundamental dynamics and the irreversibility encoded in the second law of thermodynamics. The zeroth law assumes equilibrium exists. The arrow of time and the arrow of chirality may not be independent.

04 — The Relativity of Spin

Helicity versus Chirality

Helicity is the projection of a particle's spin onto its momentum direction. If spin and momentum are parallel, the helicity is positive: right-handed. Antiparallel gives left-handed. The distinction is geometrically clear. But for massive particles, it is frame-dependent.

A massive particle moves slower than light. An observer can overtake it. In that boosted frame the momentum reverses but the spin does not. The helicity flips. What was left-handed becomes right-handed. For massive particles, helicity is an accident of perspective, not an intrinsic property.

h = sign(J⃗ · p⃗) For massless particles: no boost can reverse p⃗ → helicity is Lorentz invariant → helicity ≡ chirality

For massless particles the situation is different. Nothing outruns light. No Lorentz boost can reverse the momentum of a photon, gluon, or graviton. Their helicity is absolute. Every observer agrees. In this limit, helicity and chirality coincide. The two Greek roots — χείρ (hand) and ἕλιξ (twist) — describe the same thing.

Neutrinos: The Edge Case

Neutrinos were long assumed massless, making their left-handedness both a helicity and a chirality statement. Neutrino oscillation experiments have since shown they possess small masses. The chirality remains left-handed as a coupling property of the weak interaction. The helicity becomes frame-dependent in principle, though the corrections are negligible at observable energies.

05 — Algebraic Structure

The Poincaré Algebra and SL(2,ℂ)

Particle states are classified by the representations of the Poincaré group. This group encodes all symmetries of flat spacetime: Lorentz boosts, spatial rotations, and translations. The generators are the angular momentum operators J and the four-momentum P, closed under commutation.

[Jᵢ, Jⱼ] = iεᵢⱼₖ Jₖ Two Casimir operators: PᵘPᵤ (mass²) and WᵘWᵤ (spin) Massive: SU(2) "little group" → spin s, projections −s to +s Massless: helicity only, ±s

Two Casimir operators commute with every generator. Their eigenvalues label particle states: mass and spin. For massive particles the stabiliser of the four-momentum is SU(2), the rotation group. Spin is a familiar quantum number with 2s+1 projections. For massless particles the stabiliser is the Euclidean group E(2) and only the helicity survives as a label.

The proper orthochronous Lorentz group SO⁺(1,3) is the local symmetry of physical law. Its universal cover is the special linear group SL(2,ℂ). Pauli matrices generate rotations. Hermitian matrices generate boosts. The fundamental representation (½,0) and its conjugate (0,½) are the left and right Weyl spinors.

Dirac = Left ⊕ Right

A Dirac spinor is the direct sum of left and right Weyl components: (½,0) ⊕ (0,½). Mass couples them. In the massless limit the two halves decouple and chirality becomes a good quantum number. The chiral projection operator γ⁵ separates the two, and it is precisely this projector that appears in the weak interaction vertex.

The full Lorentz group O(1,3) has four disconnected components, distinguished by the sign of the determinant and the sign of the time-time component. Only the identity component SO⁺(1,3) is an exact symmetry. The other components contain P, T, and PT. That these are not symmetries of nature is the algebraic shadow of parity violation.

06 — The Left-Handed Force

Why the Weak Force Picks a Side

Only left-handed fermions participate in the weak interaction. This is not a tendency or a preference. It is absolute. The W and Z bosons couple exclusively to the left-chiral projection of fermion fields. Right-handed fermions are invisible to the weak force entirely.

This is frame-independent. Unlike helicity, chirality as defined by the projection operator ½(1−γ⁵) does not change under Lorentz boosts. A particle that interacts weakly does so in every reference frame. The weak interaction's handedness is written into the Lagrangian, not into the kinematics.

Electroweak Unification

The electroweak theory assigns left-handed fermions to SU(2) doublets and right-handed fermions to singlets. The asymmetry is structural: it determines how particles acquire mass through the Higgs mechanism, how generations mix, and why neutrinos produced in beta decay are always left-handed.

The question that threads through every scale returns. Why left? The medieval staircase argument shows that once a choice is made, it propagates. But the weak interaction's chirality is not a frozen accident. It is a feature of the gauge group itself. SU(2)L acts only on left-chiral fields. The subscript is not a label of convenience. It is the symmetry.

As the fireflies drawn to the light, are we not drawn to the inescapable conclusion that our chiral flesh was cast from the tumult of circularly polarised waves — the primordial evolutionary filter — the progeny of our left-fit enantiomers.
